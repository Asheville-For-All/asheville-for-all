Housing in the News

The Citizen Times published a comprehensive story this week about the homeless population in and around Asheville, specifically to dispel the too-common belief that the homeless people we see in town do not come from Western North Carolina, but rather that they are “bussed in.” The bottom line is that well more than two-thirds of the homeless people in Asheville come from Western North Carolina. And bussing is a myth.

So why is this also a story about our housing crisis?

As the article points out, the bussing myth makes it easier for people to wave away the problem of homelessness—and to demonize the homeless as outsiders or carpetbaggers—if they believe that those without homes don’t originate from our community.

At Asheville For All, we don’t believe that anyone should be without shelter—whether they’re from Western North Carolina or elsewhere. But we do need to own the fact that if families in our own neighborhoods are falling through the cracks and losing their homes, there’s something terribly wrong with the place in which we live.

It turns out that a work of research published just last year demonstrates conclusively that the degree to which a city or region sees its homeless population grow correlates with two factors: high rents, and low vacancy rates.

In short, Asheville’s housing shortage—not the bussing of outsiders—is what is at the root of our homelessness problem.
