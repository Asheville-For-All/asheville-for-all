Let’s “Make Asheville Great Again”!

Have you been following us on Twitter, Facebook, or Instagram?

In the past couple of weeks we’ve shared some slides from Eric Kronberg’s downtown Asheville presentation last month. Kronberg came from Atlanta to shares his insights on how to add density to neighborhoods in order to bring down the cost of housing.

But one slide in particular was really striking to us.

This image is taken from an old fire insurance registry. It tells us that in 1929, Asheville’s core neighborhoods—the places that are the most centrally located, walkable, and grid-like today—had a population of 30,000 people. Today, the population is almost half that much!

How does the core of a city lose half of its population, even as land values and desirability have seen no greater height than today?

The answer is that the city made intentional choices to make this land more exclusive, or, exclusionary. The result is that not only are fewer people able to take advantage of the benefits of living in walkable neighborhoods, close to downtown jobs and amenities. But also, the enforced scarcity in homes has made the land in these neighborhoods into a lucrative commodity; and the deficit of homes where they’re most in-demand has pushed up land values in a sprawling cascade that reaches out into the area’s farthest reaches.

Here’s the ironic part. Often, Asheville’s critics of adding density and multifamily homes will point to our history, and suggest that deviating from the status quo would make Asheville unrecognizable and somehow inauthentic.

This image suggests otherwise. If we want to (dare we say it) “Make Asheville Great Again,” we could do worse than making our land use rules less exclusionary!

And one way to do that is to pass “missing middle” zoning reforms, which you can learn all about at our missing middle info page.

Sign the Petition to Pass the VITAL Act

A federal piece of legislation, the VITAL Act will increase funds for the Low-Income Housing Tax Credit Program (LIHTC). The LIHTC is the number one way that income-restricted housing gets funded in the United States.

This act does something new too: it will allocate additional money, and impose certain requirements, to make sure that some of the new subsidized homes being built are accessible.

We know that Ashevilleans want to see more “affordable” homes being built for those families that are struggling. But sometimes we see rhetoric that suggests the funds for such homes can be squeezed from the wrong places. The truth is that if we want to see a more just and equitable housing landscape, we need to tell our legislators to align our public budget with our values.

You can sign the petition here. And please share!

ICYMI: Missing Middle “Open House”

In case you missed it—we’ve mentioned this event in prior newsletters, but we’ve finally received official confirmation:

The City of Asheville will be holding a “missing middle” open house and input session on August 4th, from 4pm to 7pm. (The event is “drop-in,” so you can stop by at any time in that window.) It will be downtown, at the Harrah’s Cherokee Center in the banquet room.

We hope to see you all there. The more we can show up and voice support for expansive and aggressive missing middle zoning reform, the more confidence our city’s staff and leaders will have in crafting new land use and development policies that will match the scale of our housing crisis.

Online Event THIS THURSDAY: “Funding National Affordable Housing with Diane Yentel” with YIMBY Action

Speaking of federal low income housing policy, YIMBY Action is hosting a special online event: a conversation with Diane Yentel, the President and CEO of the National Low Income Housing Coalition.

Here’s the description:

Millions of Americans struggle to keep a steady roof over their heads because of the housing shortage and affordability crisis. To truly solve these issues and achieve housing affordability and stability for all, we need to take bold steps at every level of government. At the federal level, there are lots of ways we can get involved.

From the federal budget to federal legislation focused on housing stability, Diane Yentel from the National Low Income Housing Coalition will share what we can do to advocate for housing right now across the country.

Sign up to hear more about federal affordable housing investments, as well as two key bills that could significantly increase housing stability: The Family Stability & Opportunity Vouchers Act and the Eviction Crisis Act.

This event will be on Thursday, July 27th, at 4pm. Click here to RSVP.
