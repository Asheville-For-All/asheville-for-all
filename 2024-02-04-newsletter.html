Two Steps You Can Take NOW for Housing in Asheville, Upcoming Online Event + more

February 4, 2024

ICYMI: Sign on to Support “Missing Middle” Policies That Are BROAD, AMBITIOUS, and SWIFT

Across the country (and the globe), we’ve seen cities with severe housing shortages decide to go big in removing the barriers that restrict housing supply and housing options in their core residential neighborhoods.

We’ve also seen cities implement reforms that are hamstrung by needless restrictions, for fear of disturbing the status quo.

In short, not all “missing middle” reforms are created equal. (Not sure what “missing middle” means? Click here.)

So in case you missed it, we’ve drafted a letter to call for changes that are broad, ambitious, and swift. And more than a hundred of your neighbors have signed on so far.

Have you signed yet?

City of Asheville - Online Survey

As part of the city’s goal to revise its “Affordable Housing Plan,” Asheville has posted a survey to gather input from residents as well as those who work or go to school in the city.

You can find the survey here. (Scroll down and click the button labeled “Continue” to start.)

We strongly suggest taking the short time to fill this out. Unlike last summer’s survey, it’s addressed to a wide variety of Ashevilleans—renters, owners, and families of all sizes and types.

What We’re Reading

Two essays this week caught our attention.

First, the incomparable journalist Jerusalem Demsas did a deep dive on the schism between environmentalists that recognize housing abundance and smart land use as key to resolving ecological crises, and environmentalists that are a little more behind the times. Although the essay centers around the opposition to Minneapolis’s pro-housing land use reforms, we think a lot of this article’s themes and takeaways are relevant to Asheville too.

Second, we liked a recent blog post that we discovered about the difficulty for some folks in overcoming “supply skepticism.” With a caveat that this is not written by a credentialed journalist or policy expert, we thought it did an excellent job of showing—with visuals—how restricting the construction of new multifamily homes, even when those new homes might bear a higher price tag than existing apartments, exacerbates competition among tenants and first-time homebuyers and leads to higher housing costs and to homelessness too.
